# Numerical Methods — Iterative Methods for Linear Systems

## 6.1 Why Iterate

Q: Why do practitioners use iterative methods instead of direct methods like LU?
A: Because for very large, sparse systems ($n \geq 10^6$), LU factorization costs $O(n^3)$ operations and produces fill-in that may need $O(n^2)$ memory — infeasible. Iterative methods cost $O(n)$ per iteration (assuming sparse matrix-vector products), converge in far fewer than $n$ iterations for well-conditioned problems, and never need to store or compute factors.

Q: What matrix-access pattern distinguishes iterative from direct methods?
A: Iterative methods access $A$ ONLY through matrix-vector products $\mathbf{v} \mapsto A\mathbf{v}$. The matrix never needs to be modified, stored as entries, or even stored at all — an implicit operator (e.g., a finite-difference stencil applied to a vector) suffices. Direct methods modify $A$'s entries during elimination, requiring explicit storage.

## 6.2 Stationary Iterations

C: A [stationary iteration] for $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ splits $A = M - N$ and iterates $\mathbf{x}^{(k+1)} = M^{-1}(N \mathbf{x}^{(k)} + \mathbf{b})$ — using an easy-to-invert splitting matrix $M$.

Q: When does a stationary iteration with iteration matrix $G = M^{-1} N$ converge?
A: When the [spectral radius] $\rho(G) = \max_i |\lambda_i(G)| < 1$. The error evolves as $\mathbf{e}^{(k+1)} = G \mathbf{e}^{(k)}$, so it decays geometrically with rate $\rho(G)$. Closer to zero = faster convergence; closer to $1$ = painfully slow.

## 6.3 Jacobi Iteration

Q: What is [Jacobi iteration]?
A: Take $M = D$ (diagonal of $A$). The update is $x_i^{(k+1)} = (b_i - \sum_{j \neq i} a_{ij} x_j^{(k)})/a_{ii}$ — solve each equation for its own variable using the previous iterate's values. Trivially parallel (all updates independent) but often the slowest iteration.

Q: When does Jacobi iteration converge?
A: Sufficient condition: $A$ is [strictly diagonally dominant] — $|a_{ii}| > \sum_{j \neq i}|a_{ij}|$ for every row. Also converges if $A$ is SPD, though slowly. Rate is governed by $\rho(D^{-1}(L + U))$ where $L, U$ are strict lower/upper parts. Often close to $1$, giving slow convergence.

## 6.4 Gauss–Seidel Iteration

Q: What does [Gauss–Seidel] change from Jacobi?
A: Use the MOST RECENT iterate values as soon as available: $x_i^{(k+1)} = (b_i - \sum_{j < i} a_{ij} x_j^{(k+1)} - \sum_{j > i} a_{ij} x_j^{(k)})/a_{ii}$. Splitting $M = D + L$ (diagonal + strict lower). Typically converges about twice as fast as Jacobi but is sequential — each update depends on the previous.

Q: Why does Gauss–Seidel usually beat Jacobi on the same problem?
A: Because reusing updated components immediately propagates information across the grid within each sweep, whereas Jacobi waits an entire iteration before new values are seen. For discretized PDEs, Gauss–Seidel's asymptotic convergence rate is exactly the SQUARE of Jacobi's — half as many iterations for the same accuracy.

## 6.5 SOR

C: [Successive over-relaxation (SOR)] accelerates Gauss–Seidel with a relaxation parameter $\omega$: $x_i^{(k+1)} = (1 - \omega) x_i^{(k)} + \omega \tilde{x}_i^{\text{GS}}$ where $\tilde{x}_i^{\text{GS}}$ is the Gauss–Seidel update.

Q: Why can SOR with optimal $\omega$ dramatically outperform Gauss–Seidel?
A: Because choosing $\omega \in (1, 2)$ "overshoots" each Gauss–Seidel step, accelerating convergence for problems where Gauss–Seidel consistently moves in a single direction. For 2D Poisson on an $n \times n$ grid: Gauss–Seidel needs $O(n^2)$ iterations; optimal SOR needs $O(n)$ — a factor-of-$n$ speedup.

Q: How is the optimal $\omega$ for SOR chosen?
A: For 2D Poisson: $\omega_{\text{opt}} = 2/(1 + \sin(\pi h))$ where $h$ is the grid spacing — approaches $2$ as $h \to 0$. For general problems: estimate the spectral radius of the Jacobi iteration matrix. Wrong $\omega$ (too small or too large) destroys the acceleration; adaptive SOR estimates $\omega$ during iteration.

## 6.6 Krylov Subspaces

C: The [Krylov subspace] of dimension $k$ generated by $A$ and $\mathbf{b}$ is $\mathcal{K}_k(A, \mathbf{b}) = \text{span}\{\mathbf{b}, A\mathbf{b}, A^2 \mathbf{b}, \dots, A^{k-1}\mathbf{b}\}$.

Q: Why are Krylov subspaces the natural home of iterative methods?
A: Because they are exactly the information accessible by matrix-vector products alone: after $k$ products with $A$, any vector you could have built sits in $\mathcal{K}_k$. Krylov methods approximate $\mathbf{x}$ by the best element of $\mathcal{K}_k(A, \mathbf{b})$ under various optimality criteria — conjugate gradient minimizes $A$-norm error; GMRES minimizes residual norm.

## 6.7 Conjugate Gradient

C: Conjugate gradient (CG) solves $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ for SPD $A$ by iteratively minimizing the quadratic $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{x}$ along [$A$-conjugate directions].

Q: Why does conjugate gradient converge in at most $n$ iterations in exact arithmetic?
A: Because the iterates lie in $\mathcal{K}_k(A, \mathbf{b})$ which grows one dimension per iteration; after $n$ iterations, $\mathcal{K}_n = \mathbb{R}^n$, forcing the optimum. In practice, roundoff and finite precision prevent exact termination — but convergence to tolerance usually happens in far fewer than $n$ steps.

Q: What determines CG's convergence rate in practice?
A: The distribution of eigenvalues of $A$. For condition number $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$: error bound $\|\mathbf{e}_k\|_A \leq 2 \left(\frac{\sqrt{\kappa} - 1}{\sqrt{\kappa} + 1}\right)^k \|\mathbf{e}_0\|_A$. Clustered eigenvalues converge even faster. $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ iterations for fixed accuracy — much better than stationary methods' $O(\kappa)$.

Q: What memory does CG use per iteration?
A: Only a handful of length-$n$ vectors: current iterate $\mathbf{x}_k$, residual $\mathbf{r}_k$, search direction $\mathbf{p}_k$, and temporary $A\mathbf{p}_k$. This constant-memory property makes CG the dominant method for huge SPD systems — storing only $O(n)$ scalars regardless of iteration count.

## 6.8 GMRES

Q: What is [GMRES] and when is it used instead of CG?
A: [Generalized Minimal Residual] for NON-symmetric $A$. Constructs an orthonormal basis of $\mathcal{K}_k(A, \mathbf{r}_0)$ via Arnoldi iteration; picks the $\mathbf{x}_k \in \mathbf{x}_0 + \mathcal{K}_k$ minimizing $\|\mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}_k\|_2$. Always converges monotonically in residual norm; workhorse for Navier–Stokes, electromagnetics, and general nonsymmetric PDE solvers.

Q: Why is GMRES typically [restarted]?
A: Because memory grows linearly with iteration count (storing the full Arnoldi basis), and each iteration does orthogonalization against all previous vectors at cost $O(nk)$. "Restarted GMRES($m$)" runs $m$ iterations, uses the result as a new starting point, and repeats — bounding memory and per-iteration cost. Convergence may stall if $m$ is too small.

## 6.9 BiCGStab and MINRES

Q: What does [BiCGStab] solve, and how does it differ from GMRES?
A: Like GMRES, it handles nonsymmetric $A$. Uses short recurrences (constant memory per iteration) unlike GMRES's growing basis. Trades GMRES's monotonic residual for cheaper iterations — convergence can be erratic but often wins in total cost. Good fit when GMRES restart length is too constraining.

Q: When is [MINRES] used?
A: For symmetric but INDEFINITE systems (e.g., KKT matrices from constrained optimization, saddle-point problems). CG fails on indefinite systems; MINRES minimizes residual norm over Krylov subspaces using short recurrences. Combines CG-like cheap iterations with GMRES-like residual minimization, exploiting symmetry.

## 6.10 Preconditioning

C: A [preconditioner] $M \approx A$ accelerates a Krylov method by solving $M^{-1} A \mathbf{x} = M^{-1} \mathbf{b}$ — giving $M^{-1}A$ a better eigenvalue distribution than $A$.

Q: Why is preconditioning often the difference between a usable and unusable iterative solver?
A: Because convergence scales with $\kappa(M^{-1}A)$, not $\kappa(A)$. Many practical systems have $\kappa(A) \sim 10^{10}$, giving CG $\sim 10^5$ iterations. A good preconditioner reduces effective $\kappa$ to $O(1)$, giving $O(1)$ iterations — orders-of-magnitude speedup. "Iterative solver = matrix operator + good preconditioner."

Q: What is an [incomplete LU] (ILU) preconditioner?
A: Compute an approximate LU factorization of $A$ that discards fill-in entries (or entries below a threshold), producing sparse approximate factors $\tilde{L}, \tilde{U}$ with $\tilde{L}\tilde{U} \approx A$. Apply $M^{-1}$ via cheap triangular solves. "ILU(0)" keeps only fill in the sparsity pattern of $A$; "ILU(p)" allows $p$ levels of fill. General-purpose workhorse preconditioner.

Q: Why is a [multigrid] method a near-optimal preconditioner for elliptic PDEs?
A: Because it attacks error on all scales: smoothers (Jacobi, Gauss–Seidel) kill high-frequency error on the fine grid; coarse-grid corrections kill low-frequency error cheaply. Total cost per multigrid V-cycle: $O(n)$ operations to reduce error by a fixed factor. Mesh-independent convergence rate — the holy grail for PDE solvers.

## 6.11 Stopping Criteria

Q: What are sensible stopping criteria for a Krylov solver?
A: Combine: (i) relative residual $\|\mathbf{r}_k\|/\|\mathbf{b}\| < \tau$ (most common), (ii) maximum iteration count as safety cap, (iii) stagnation detection ($\|\mathbf{r}_k\|$ not decreasing). For ill-conditioned problems, small residual does not imply small error — augment with condition number estimates when stakes are high.

## 6.12 Practical Guidelines

Q: How do you choose between direct and iterative solvers?
A: Use [direct] for moderate $n$ ($\leq 10^5$ in 3D or $\leq 10^6$ in 2D), multiple right-hand sides with same $A$, or dense $A$. Use [iterative] for huge sparse systems, matrix-free operators (stencil-based, Jacobian-free Newton–Krylov), or when only approximate solutions are needed. Hybrid: use a coarse direct solve as preconditioner for iterative.

Q: Which Krylov method should be used for an SPD matrix?
A: [CG] (conjugate gradient).

Q: Which Krylov method should be used for a symmetric indefinite matrix?
A: [MINRES].

Q: Which Krylov method should be used for a nonsymmetric matrix when memory is available?
A: [GMRES (restarted)].

Q: Which Krylov method should be used for a nonsymmetric matrix under tight memory constraints?
A: [BiCGStab].

Q: What matters more than the choice of Krylov method for iterative solving?
A: Good preconditioning (ILU, multigrid, domain decomposition).
